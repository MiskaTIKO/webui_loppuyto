Musikaalisia tuotoksia

Tässä on viime kesänä tekemäni Sound & Music for Games -opintojakson tuotokset. Kuvatekstit on otettu suoraan videoiden kuvauksista ja ovat siksi englanniksi.

For this assignment I used Fruity Loops Studio. All of the sounds, including music were created with foley sounds. I recorded multiple different sounds, like knocking the package of my old CPU fan, heavy breathing, radio static, scraping two kitchen knives against each other, and the squeaking of my butterfly comb. After recording the samples, I used various filters and effects like bitcrush, ring, lo-fi, reverb, delay and some EQing as well. And yes, I also dubbed the parts with voice acting myself. This was such a fun assignment! It was interesting to figure out what kind of sounds could be used for different use cases. For example, for the part where Harry, the player character lights up a torch, I crumpled a small plastic bag into my fist and rubbed it with my other hand, after which I applied effects to make it sound nice. The sound didn't turn out to be exactly the way I wanted, but I think it still works really nicely. My main goal was not to make everything sound super realistic, but believable. I actually started the whole project with the "monster chase" track. I really wanted to emulate that Akira Yamaoka feel and turns out foley sounds were THE way to go. Again I want to reiterate that I did not use any instruments here. Only recorded and sampled sounds. It was really fun.
Music and sound created by me. Gameplay footage is from Silent Hill on the Sony Playstation. The gameplay footage is from MadOverdose's YouTube channel. I do not own the game, nor anything related to it. Game copyrights belong to Konami.

For this assignment I wanted to make a boss battle theme for Chrono Trigger. The idea for the melody actually came to me when I was taking a shower yesterday. It's not the first time I come up with someting cool while taking long refreshing shower, but this time I actually remembered to write the thing down in FL Studio. The original idea sounded very orchestral in my head, but I don't own any good orchestra VSTs, which is why I decided to go with soundfonts from several SNES and PSX games. The soundfonts used are from Chrono Trigger (surprise), Chrono Cross, MegaMan X, Seiken Densetsu 3 and Final Fantasy 7. The workflow was pretty straightforward. Not much to say, really. I played the original melody on piano, started adding chords that sound nice and then began building and adding to the track in FL Studio. It took some time to figure out what kinds of instruments I wanted to use. I'm not a professional, or even an amateur composer to be completely frank, so I think I did a pretty good job! A string like sound plays the main melody, very characteristic to JRPG battle themes. For the chords I went with a sound similar to a church organ, because it sounds very serious, holy and sinister. I also just REALLY love the organ as an instrument. I used a slap bass sound from the MegaMan X soundfont for the bassline to give the track some warmth and rhythm, which is accompanied by some simple drums and percussions. At around 51 seconds is a little "break" from the more hectic part of the track. I'm not sure what the term for this part of the track would be. Please don't kill me! I cut the drums and bassline to make it a bit more peaceful. I added some heavenly bell sounds and took the melody and chord progression to more melancholic direction. Then the drums kick back in and the track starts over. I have a lot of song and soundtrack ideas, and I've started many projects, but I rarely sit my butt down and actually finish what I'm doing. I've never "finished" a track before. So in that regard this was a huge personal accomplishment for me. I grew up listening to video game music and I really want to compose stuff in the future.

First things first. I completely misread the instructions for this assignment and ended up making music for this scene instead of a soundscape. I'm so sorry. I spent the entire day soing this assignment and I really have to sleep before tomorrow's presentations, so I'll do a proper soundscape soon. Maybe during the weekend. But I also didn't want to completely discard today's work, so I'll upload this as it is. The song consists of one organ-like synth made with Native Instrument's Massive and two noise tracks, one being white noise and the other being "paper" noise from the Massive plugin. I also used a bit of reverb. At first I was going to go for drone, as the scene is quite slow and mysterious. I made a sine wave synth and applied a subtle LFO filter to give it some "wob wobs". It sounded good, but I ended up wanting something different. I wanted something a bit more "musical". Since the video footage doesn't change all that much, I decided to make the track somewhat repetitive, yet progressive, so that it doesn't get too boring. My first question was: "What emotions does this scene evoke in me?" To me it's kind of dark and sad, so i created a synth that sounds kind of like an organ and started with two chords. These two chords repeat throughout the song, with some variations here and there, as well as some melody too. As the camera keeps moving forward, progressing through whatever is submerged in the water, the song also progresses with it. The noise tracks kick in and the song evolves forward. As the camera approaches the hand of the person lying in the water, the white noise intensifies. And when the hand is finally in the center of the screen, the music loses all tension, like it's telling "this person is most likely dead." As the camera pans out, the high note dims away. Despite my misunderstanding the assignment, I'm quite happy about the way this track turned out to be. I've never made anything like this, so it was a joyous experiment. As I stated earlier, I'll return a properly done soundscape in the near future.
I only created the audio for this clip. Copyrights to the video footage of the film Stalker (1979) belong to Mosfilm studio.

As a part of my Final Boss quest, I wanted to make a track for Armored Core. The creation of the track started of with simple drums and the acid synth arpeggio. I added a rhythmic bassline that has an analog feel to it. One of my favorite tracks from the series is "Circulation" from the first Armored Core game for the Playstation, so I in a way wanted to make my own version of it. The choir melody is more or less the same as in "Circulation", but played from a different key with a different tempo. For the choir I used a soundfont from Chrono Trigger. I downloaded a clip of some police radio chatter from freesound.org and added some reverb and tweaked the EQ to make it inaudible. I feel like I learned a bit more about mixing with this one. It's not perfect by any means, but I really like how it turned out.

Town themes in JRPGs are quite important, as the player often spends a lot of time in them, buying all kinds of items and equipment. I wasn't supposed to make a town theme initially, but after humming some funny tune in the shower a few weeks back, I couldn't resist the urge. The track has a total of 4 instruments: music box, pizzicato strings, regular strings, and panflute. Funnily enough, the panflute sounds almost like an organ when they play a chord. All of the instruments are from soundfonts from different games. The music box is from Final Fantasy V, the pizzcato from Trials of Mana, the panflute from Chrono Trigger and the regular strings are from FL Studio's own soundfont player. Despite this track being rather simple, it had quite a long development process. I really had to figure out what instruments go well with each other and the overall structure of the song had a couple of revisions. At first it was just the music box and pizzicato strings. I was stuck with them for a while, until I threw in the panflute and the strings followed. I think the track is very warm and joyful. I was going to record me running around in the city of Lindblum in Final Fantasy IX, but I stumpled upon this nice gif of one of the pre-rendered backgrounds. Out of all the towns and cities I know from JRPGs, Lindblum feels like the best fit. I had some difficulties with the mixing and for some reason the FL Soundfont Player screwed up the music box, it sounded muffled after exporting the audio file. But with some quick googling and patience, I found the TX16Wx Software Sampler and that fixed my music box issue. I had a ton of fun with the track. It was nice to do something I haven't ever tried before.

My original plan was to make three different tracks: some kind of a JRPG track, a sci-fi track and a horror track. I ended up discarding horror idea altogether and decided to expand on my previous Etude assignment, the boss battle track for Chrono Trigger. While I was happy with the original track I had created, I felt it was lacking something. I know there's no such thing as a "complete" piece of art, but still I felt like I could spark some magic to it if I wanted to. And I definitely wanted. I wanted to make the track sound more grandiose and epic. I replaced the slap bass with a timpani drum and added a rhythmic hihat to accompany the timpani. This change already gives the track a more traditional and organic battle theme feel. I also added a harp arpeggio, which makes the verse sound more full and exciting. The verse repeats two times before the chorus. On the second repeat, the strings start playing two-note chords and there's a subtle yet noticable deviation in the melody, which all add a bit of oomph to make the second repeat more exciting. The chorus (I think? I'm not entirely sure how song structure terms work in this kind of music.) now has a choir, which makes it sound rather heavenly and it definitely gives the chorus some much needed color. I had to adjust the panning of the bell sound make it stand out from all the other instruments. After the chorus, the drums return and the harp arpeggio plays for a while to sort of connect the track back to where it started. In terms of mixing, this was a really challeninging track. Not only did I want to mix the original instruments better, I now had to take the new instruments to consideration. Mixing is definitely more harder when there are multiple instruments playing, especially if they all play a chord of some sort. It can end up sounding very muddy, and we don't want that. The mixing here isn't perfect, but I don't think it's terrible either. I tried my best to separate the instruments with different panning positions. Mixing is still very tricky for me, but I think this little challenge here gave me some idea of how it should work. Out of all the assignments I've done during this course, I've spent the most hours with this one. It is my first, actually "complete" track and I plan to keep it close to my heart.
